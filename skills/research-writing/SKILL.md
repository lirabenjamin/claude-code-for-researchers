SKILL — Research Writing

## Purpose

Help me write clear, engaging, high-level scientific prose that works for both experts and non-experts.

Core principle:

**Other people matter.**  
Write for the reader, not for the writer.

---

## 1. Core Writing Philosophy

- Writing is craft, not talent.
- Clarity beats sophistication.
- The goal is understanding, not performance.
- Scientific writing is still writing.

When in doubt:

- make it clearer
- make it shorter
- make it more concrete

---

## 2. Voice & Style Rules

### Active voice

Prefer:

- “Students completed questionnaires.”

Avoid:

- “Questionnaires were completed.”

Use passive voice only when the actor truly does not matter.

---

### Strong topic sentences

Every paragraph should begin with a clear topic sentence that states the main idea.

Think:

- topic sentences = ladder rungs
- reader should understand the argument by skimming first sentences

Avoid unnecessary suspense at the paragraph level.

---

### Short sentences when possible

- One idea per sentence.
- Split long sentences aggressively.
- If you need to take a breath while reading aloud, cut it.

---

### Show, don’t tell

Prefer concrete detail over abstract description.

Bad:

- “Participants were highly engaged.”

Better:

- “Participants kept typing after the timer ended.”

Use examples, quotes, or specific behaviors whenever possible.

---

### Be specific about people

Never write vague nouns like “people” if specificity exists.

Replace with:

- students
- writers
- participants
- consumers
- forecasters
- teachers
- users

Always ask: *who exactly?*

---

## 3. The Seven Writing Sins (Avoid)

1. Needless words  
2. Jargon (unless necessary and explained)  
3. Acronyms (use sparingly)  
4. Passive voice  
5. Telling instead of showing  
6. Weak or delayed main points  
7. No narrative tension

---

## 4. Concision Rules

- Omit needless words.
- Cut aggressively during revision.
- Replace phrases with single words when possible.
- Remove throat-clearing.

Useful mindset:

> When in doubt, cut it out.

Assume first drafts are too long.

---

## 5. Jargon & Accessibility

- Avoid field-specific shorthand unless essential.
- Explain terms briefly when used.
- Prefer plain language without dumbing down.

Rule:

Write so an intelligent outsider could follow.

---

## 6. Structure & Suspense

At the paper level:

- Introduction sets question and stakes.
- Present competing explanations or uncertainty.
- Results resolve tension.

Narrative arc matters even in scientific writing.

---

## 7. Reader Empathy

Assume the reader is:

- smart
- busy
- tired
- unfamiliar with your exact subfield

Make reading easy:

- clear structure
- fast payoff
- minimal friction

---

## 8. Drafting vs Revising

### Drafting

- Ignore polish.
- Get ideas onto the page.
- Clarity comes later.

### Revising

- Rewrite for the reader.
- Move key sentences upward.
- Strengthen topic sentences.
- Cut heavily.

---

## 9. Read-Aloud Rule

Read important writing aloud.

When reading aloud, look for:

- awkward rhythm
- long sentences
- repeated words
- unclear logic

If it sounds wrong, rewrite.

---

## 10. Voice

Aim to sound like a real human:

- clear
- confident
- conversational when appropriate

Avoid:

- overly formal stiffness
- robotic academic tone

Writing should have personality without losing rigor.

---

## 11. Scientific Writing Defaults

- Mechanism before description.
- Concrete examples before abstraction.
- Claims should be specific and testable.
- Avoid exaggeration.

---

## 12. Editing Checklist (Run Automatically)

Before finalizing writing:

- Is the first sentence of each paragraph strong?
- Can any sentence be shorter?
- Are actors explicit (who did what)?
- Is jargon necessary?
- Are examples concrete?
- Does each paragraph advance the argument?
- Could a reader skim topic sentences and understand the story?

---

## 13. Preferred Output Behavior

When helping me write:

- rewrite rather than lightly edit if clarity improves
- suggest cuts aggressively
- strengthen topic sentences
- replace passive constructions
- replace abstract statements with specific ones
- prioritize readability over stylistic flourish

---

## 14. Guiding Principle

Good scientific writing is not about sounding smart.

It is about helping the reader think clearly.

Write so the reader never has to work harder than necessary.
